01. The Problem

A visible image. A hidden identity.

Wolfsburg is known worldwide for Volkswagen, mobility, and modern planning. But people have lived ordinary, meaningful lives here for generations: arrivals, work, childhoods, family, waiting, walking. That side rarely becomes part of the city's visible identity.

Official Image Volkswagen, factories, cars, mobility, planning
Hidden Everyday Identity Arrival, work, childhood, family, waiting, public life

Wolfsburg's identity needs to grow from an industrial image into a living one.

02. Where The Idea Started

Bottling Memories.

The idea started with a bottle. Not as a nostalgic object, but as a simple example of something that holds a fragment of a moment and keeps it from disappearing.

Memo-Haus imagines the city the same way: a living archive that holds scattered memories, photographs, voices, stories, routes, waiting moments, and everyday experiences that are usually invisible in public space.

These fragments are collected from people, anchored to specific locations in Wolfsburg, and reconstructed as spatial 3D experiences, preserved like an archive, but experienced as part of the city's shared identity.

Memo-Haus preserves scattered memories as a living spatial archive.

03. The Concept

Memory becomes a living archive.

Memo-Haus collects the everyday memories of Wolfsburg: arrivals, work routines, childhoods, family stories, waiting, walking, the ordinary moments usually missing from the city's public image.

Each photo, voice note, and story is tied to a place, a time, and a theme, then rebuilt as a 3D spatial memory using Gaussian splatting.

Inside the pavilion, these reconstructions form a living archive that keeps growing and branching as more memories are added, rather than a fixed collection.

05. Solving Wolfsburg's Identity Problem

One industrial image. Many lived ones.

Most people know Wolfsburg through Volkswagen, mobility, and modern planning. But underneath that image are quieter memories that exist all over the city and almost never get acknowledged: arriving for the first time, growing up here, starting a first job, waiting for family, feeling lonely, or celebrating at a local festival.

Memo-Haus ties real, contributed memories to exact locations and layers them across a timeline, so a single place, the pavilion site at the start of Porschestraße, can show how its meaning has shifted from one generation to the next. Put together, these scattered personal memories form a shared spatial record, one that surfaces Wolfsburg's everyday lived identity next to its industrial one, not instead of it, but alongside it.

07a. The Site

Right where the city already moves.

The pavilion sits right at the start of Porschestraße, the city's main pedestrian street, at the junction next to the central bus station (ZOB) and a short walk from the Hauptbahnhof.

That position was chosen on purpose. Bus routes converge here, and commuters and shoppers pass through it every day, so the site already carries more footfall than almost anywhere else in Wolfsburg. The pavilion doesn't need to attract an audience; it just needs to meet the one already passing through.

It's also a threshold in a more pointed sense: this is the entrance to a street named after Ferdinand Porsche, the symbolic edge of the city's industrial identity. Placing an everyday-memory pavilion exactly there puts the hidden, lived side of Wolfsburg right at the doorstep of the image that usually overshadows it.

High footfall, a real transit junction, and a symbolic threshold, all in one site.

07b. The Pavilion

Where memories come to life.

Memo-Haus is built as a layered memory capsule. The outer shell is a 200mm thick, dark GFRC wall with a blind-perforated surface, holes recessed rather than fully open, so the wall reads as porous from outside while still blocking daylight. Its inner face is lined in matte black acoustic panelling, forming a dark, protective threshold around the pavilion.

Inside that shell, the corridor between the outer wall and the translucent display cylinder is the first part of the experience. Visitors don't reach the memory field immediately; they walk through a dim circular passage where fragments of the display glow faintly through the inner screen's outer surface. The corridor slows them down, separates them from the city outside, and prepares them for the memory chamber ahead.

The inner cylinder itself is curved, laminated, translucent safety glass with an integrated transparent LED mesh and an anti-glare diffusion film. It's the main display surface, showing photos, text, particles, and Gaussian-splatted 3D memory scenes. Because it's translucent, memories can be sensed from the corridor as soft traces, then experienced from inside as a full spatial environment.

At the centre, a steel service core wrapped in vertical timber cladding forms the tree-shaped column, housing the media server, sensors, sound, lighting, and ventilation. Symbolically, the tree roots the memories in Wolfsburg and branches them outward into the glowing display. It also does the structural work of holding up the roof.

The roof is a steel-framed canopy clad in dark fibre-cement, with a black acoustic underside. It shades the interior, hides the technical systems above, and reinforces the pavilion's dark, atmospheric character.

If footfall at this first site proves the idea, the same pavilion doesn't have to stay a one-off. Its cylindrical form is already close to a familiar piece of German street furniture, the Litfaßsäule, advertising columns placed deliberately at the city's busiest corners. Memo-Haus could spread the same way: a small family of these memory columns, each one set down at a high-footfall point across Wolfsburg, rather than a single landmark everyone has to travel to.

07c. Navigation

Two ways to move through the memories.

Browse on your phone, or on the pavilion's shared touch display, either way you're steering the same particle-based, Gaussian-splatted memory field.

Phone: scan the QR code, browse by map, timeline, theme, or archive, then send a memory to the main display and swipe, pinch, or tilt to move through it.

Touch display: a shared, public version of the same controls, right on the pavilion's translucent cylinder.

Only one scene plays at a time. Everyone else queues, votes, or browses privately. Left alone, the pavilion just keeps cycling through memories on its own.

07b. The Physical Mockup

Testing the form before going digital.

A small physical mockup tests the pavilion's shape, scale, and material feel before committing to the final design, the curved walls, the entry sequence, how light moves through the form, and how the space actually feels at human scale.

It also tests the translucent cylinder itself: memories stay visible from inside while softly glowing through to the outside, so passersby catch a hint of what's playing before they step in.

A quick, simple bridge between the concept sketch and the digital pavilion.
